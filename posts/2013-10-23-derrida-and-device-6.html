Being a discourse in furnished on

Recently, I reviewed DEVICE 6 for Kill Screen, and it was all I could do to limit myself to a barely-noticed link to a Derrida quotation. The experience of the game as a game was great, and for the most part, I don’t think one needs a background in critical theory to play it. But for me, I think it’s much more interesting as a text—which, I guess, is the whole point.

Sequined Parachute Pants

One of my favorite screwers-around-with-form, John D’Agata, gave a reading at CSUF while I was in grad school, and at the Q&A beforehand he talked about the problems with experimental form. Most of the submissions he got at Seneca were experimental for no particular reason—or rather, were experimental for the sake of being experimental. If the form isn’t integral to the mechanism of the piece—if it could be accomplished with a traditional form, if the piece didn’t need the formal manipulations—it generally reads like a ridiculous-looking bid for attention. Like, he suggested, a man wearing sequined parachute pants.

I think of that a lot when I’m trying weird stuff, which is usually. In fact, with the D6 review, I was originally planning on having the Rereading section end with a link back to the top of the article, after which more detail would reveal itself, changing the focus of the text. But ultimately I felt like that would be good, useful, interesting, and supportive of the text—but not necessary.1

As I’ve continued to think about the formal experiments in D6, I’ve become increasingly convinced that there are really two different manipulations at work. One of them, I think, is necessary. The other is … complicated.

Interstices

The first major manipulation of form in D6 is the one my review focused on: the interstitial interactions between the player and HAT. To sum up the review, at first, those interactions feel extraneous, and in particular it’s hard to tell what matters. After the first chapter, you’re asked some questions about obscure details from the chapter, and you wonder what happens if you get those questions right or wrong. At the conclusion of the questions, you receive a score—100 points, in my case—and it is not clear what, if anything, that score means. As the game approaches its conclusion, it becomes obvious that it is not Simogo asking those questions, but HAT, and that these lags in the game are integral to its story.

The questions and tasks that those interstitial surveys present you with are great in and of themselves, and are full of subtle, smirk-inspiring jabs at the corporate culture whose voice they co-opt. (In one section, you respond by answering questions on a scale of 1-5 hearts; the final question in the section is, “For administrative purposes, please mark five hearts.”) It’s those moments that make the game play like a focus group for a game, rather than a game itself, and those interactions remove you from the reality of the story—but the reality they remove you into isn’t exactly your own.

This is the mechanism that is, I think, essential for the game to work. It does a lot of work in setting the tone of the game, in keeping the player unsettled, and especially in reframing the player’s role and identity as the story progresses.

Dynamic Text

This is the part I’m less sure about. On the one hand, without D6‘s excursions into nonlinear text, there really wouldn’t be much game there. I think iOS is a perfect place to do this kind of thing, and I think D6 does it beautifully. And I haven’t seen anything quite like it, either. It’s the interplay of image, sound, text, and movement that makes the game what it is—at least in terms of gameplay—and I’m glad that Simogo is willing to take this kind of leap. It’s excellently done and welcome in the market—but Iâ€™m not sure whether it’s necessary.

The notion of nonlinear text is not a new one by any means, and in fact, it’s more or less the concept the web is built on. And in fact—as anyone who’s searched Wikipedia for the French Foreign Legion and come up for air seven hours later at the list of serial killers by number of victims can tell you—the divergent, fractal nature of hypertext is a much more powerful deviation from the traditional linear format than D6, by a long shot. The web is like a Choose-Your-Own-Adventure where any given page can be both an entry point or an ending, as well as just another fork on a road. Forgive me for this, but it allows infinite diversity in infinite combination.
